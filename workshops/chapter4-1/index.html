    Topic Modeling and Analysis: Finding Patterns in User Feedback

    This Is My Favorite Session

    Let me be straight with you - this is probably my favorite session in the entire course. Why? Because this is where everything clicks. This is where you stop randomly tweaking embeddings and start systematically improving your application.

    The general ideas we're covering are about how to segment the data coming from your users and customers, and most importantly - understanding when to double down and when to fold. This is fundamentally how you improve your application.

    The Problem: Too Much Feedback, Not Enough Insight

    So you deployed your RAG system and added feedback collection. Great. Now you've got thousands of queries, ratings, and signals. Your manager asks "What should we improve next?" and you realize you have no idea.

    This happened to me. We had tons of data but no systematic way to find patterns. Looking at individual bad ratings wasn't helping - we needed to see the bigger picture.

    The Analysis Paralysis Trap

    I worked with a B2B SaaS company that had 2 million queries logged, beautiful dashboards showing 68% average satisfaction, and absolutely no clue what to fix. Their engineers were randomly tweaking embeddings while their biggest customer was about to churn because date-based queries never worked. The data was all there - they just weren't looking at it the right way.

    Here's the thing that took me way too long to figure out: aggregate metrics lie. Your 70% satisfaction score might hide the fact that new users have a 20% success rate on their first query. Or that your biggest customer segment is failing 80% of the time on a specific query type.

    The solution? Stop looking at averages and start looking at segments. Topic modeling and clustering aren't fancy ML techniques - they're just ways to group similar things together so you can see patterns. Once you do this, the problems become obvious.

    I remember the moment this clicked for me. We were working with a legal tech company, and they kept saying "our search just isn't good enough." We clustered their queries and found something shocking: contract review queries had 89% satisfaction, but litigation research queries had 23% satisfaction. Same system, wildly different results. Turns out they'd been optimizing for the wrong thing the entire time.

    Prerequisites and context

    • Read: 3.1 Feedback Collection, 3.2 Overcoming Latency
    • You’ll use feedback and logs to segment queries and discover improvement opportunities

    Learning objectives

    • Build actionable segments from feedback and queries
    • Prioritize segments using volume × satisfaction and business value
    • Stand up a simple classification pipeline that’s production-ready

    Metrics and evaluation

    • Segment coverage: % of queries in top 10–20 segments; size of "other"
    • Satisfaction by segment; volume × satisfaction quadrant movement
    • Classifier accuracy and confidence trends; drift alerts
    • Retry-rate and time-to-success improvements per segment after fixes

    Common pitfalls and anti-patterns

    • Over-segmentation (50+ categories) leading to paralysis
    • Ignoring cohort/time dynamics (survivorship bias)
    • Skipping manual review before clustering
    • No "other" category to capture emerging patterns

    Think of it like being a detective. You've got all these clues (queries, feedback, user behavior), but they don't make sense until you organize them. Once you do, the story practically tells itself.

    Why Segmentation Matters: The Marketing Analogy

    Let me explain this with something we all understand: marketing. I was at Stitch Fix when we had this exact problem.

    We ran this big marketing campaign and sales jumped 80%. Everyone's celebrating, right? But nobody knew why. Was it the Instagram influencers? The podcast ads? The referral program we'd just launched?

    You could imagine that you're selling a consumer product and you run a marketing campaign to boost sales. And as a result of your efforts, maybe there's an 80 percent boost in sales, but you don't really know why. Alternatively, you could also have an 80 percent drop in sales, and you also wouldn't know why.

    So we dug into the data as data scientists and analysts do. And here's what blew my mind: 60% of the increase came from one specific segment - professional women aged 30-45 in the Midwest. Not the coastal cities where we'd been focusing. The Midwest.

    Once we identified this, what we could do then is use this information and decide - is this an audience that we want to invest more efforts into? How could we target this audience better? Maybe there are podcasts where the demographic covers exactly what we're looking for.

    Instead of blasting generic ads everywhere or running Super Bowl ads, we started sponsoring career-focused podcasts. We partnered with LinkedIn on professional styling content. We even adjusted our inventory to stock more business-casual options that this segment loved.

    The result? Our marketing ROI tripled. Not because we got smarter about marketing - because we got smarter about who we were marketing to.

    The Power of Knowing Your Segments

    At Stitch Fix, segmentation revealed that Midwest professionals were 3x more likely to become long-term subscribers than our average customer. But they had different needs - they wanted work-appropriate clothing, not trendy weekend wear. One insight changed our entire strategy.

    This is exactly what happens with RAG applications. You think you have "users" but really you have segments with totally different needs.

    I learned this the hard way at a fintech company. We thought our RAG system was pretty good - 75% satisfaction overall. Then we segmented by user role and discovered:

    • Analysts asking technical questions: 91% satisfaction
    • Executives asking strategic questions: 42% satisfaction
    • Customer support asking policy questions: 78% satisfaction

    We'd been celebrating our success with analysts while our executives - the people who decided whether to renew our contract - were getting garbage results. Oops.

    The lesson? Your average user doesn't exist. You have segments, and they want different things. Once you see this, you can actually fix the right problems.

    The Four-Quadrant Framework and Expected Value

    Alright, here's where things get interesting. Once you segment your queries, you need a simple way to decide what to fix first.

    Let me give you the formula that's fundamentally changed how I think about improvements:

    Expected Value = Impact × % of queries × P(Success)

    This is fundamentally how you improve your application. By breaking down your application using this formula, you can figure out what lever is the most important to tune. It's no longer just "making the AI better" - it's identifying certain segments that matter to you.

    Now, let me show you how to visualize this with a simple 2x2 matrix that makes priorities obvious:

    Picture a graph. Vertical axis is satisfaction (how well you handle queries). Horizontal axis is volume (how often users ask). This gives you four quadrants, and each one tells you exactly what to do.

    Top Right: High Volume, High Satisfaction This is your bread and butter. Lots of users asking, you're nailing the answers. Don't mess with it. But don't ignore it either.

    I worked with a legal tech company where "find cases citing [statute]" queries were here - 30% of all queries, 85% satisfaction. Their mistake? They kept trying to "improve" this instead of focusing on problem areas. If it ain't broke...

    Top Left: Low Volume, High Satisfaction These are your hidden gems. You're amazing at something users don't know about.

    My favorite example: an HR system that could brilliantly compare benefits across office locations. The feature was incredible - it would pull data from multiple sources, normalize everything, even account for cost-of-living differences. Only problem? Just 2% of users knew they could ask.

    The fix was simple. We added example queries to the search bar: "Try: Compare Seattle vs Austin office benefits." Usage jumped 10x in a month.

    Bottom Left: Low Volume, Low Satisfaction Be honest here. These are often edge cases that would take massive effort to fix for minimal impact.

    One client wanted to support questions like "Generate a Gantt chart showing dependencies between all contracts expiring in Q3 sorted by liability." Cool idea. Would take 3 engineers 2 months to build. Would help maybe 5 users per month. We said no.

    Bottom Right: High Volume, Low Satisfaction This is your hair-on-fire emergency. Lots of users asking, terrible results. Fix this NOW.

    The Danger Zone

    A SaaS analytics company discovered that 35% of their queries were date comparisons ("What changed since last quarter?") with only 25% satisfaction. Users were so frustrated they were exporting to Excel instead. After fixing date filtering, satisfaction jumped to 78% and Excel exports dropped 60%. One fix saved the product.

    The beauty of this framework? It ends arguments. No more "I think we should build X" or "customers keep asking for Y." Just plot everything on the matrix and the priorities become obvious.

    Implementing Topic Modeling

    Now let's get practical. How do you actually segment thousands of queries into meaningful groups?

    Look, I'm not going to lie - the first time I tried this, I made it way too complicated. I was using fancy clustering algorithms, tweaking hyperparameters, the whole nine yards. Total waste of time.

    Here's what actually works:

    Start Simple with Manual Categories

    Before you do any fancy ML, just read through a few hundred queries. I know, I know - that sounds terrible. But trust me, you'll learn more in 2 hours of reading actual queries than in 2 weeks of tweaking clustering algorithms.

    When I did this for a healthcare company, patterns jumped out immediately:

    • Medication questions always included drug names
    • Procedure questions used words like "how to" or "steps"
    • Insurance questions mentioned "coverage" or "cost"
    • Side effect questions included "caused by" or "reaction to"

    Sometimes the obvious approach is the right approach.

    The Semi-Automated Sweet Spot

    Once you have initial categories, then you can get fancy. Here's my process:

    Step 1: Embed Everything Take all your queries and convert them to embeddings. Any embedding model works - I usually start with whatever OpenAI or Cohere offers. Don't overthink it.

    Step 2: Cluster and Inspect Run k-means clustering with maybe 20-30 clusters. Too few and you miss nuances. Too many and you can't see patterns.

    Here's the critical part: look at the queries closest to each cluster center. These are your "most representative" examples. If you can't immediately see what a cluster represents, it's probably not a useful segment.

    Step 3: Name and Refine Give each cluster a human-readable name. If you can't name it, merge it with something else or throw it in an "other" bucket.

    I once spent days trying to interpret a cluster that turned out to be typos and gibberish. Learn from my mistakes.

    The Classification Pipeline

    Once you know your segments, you need to classify new queries in real-time. Here's the approach that's worked for me:

    Build a simple few-shot classifier. Take 5-10 good examples from each segment, throw them in a prompt, and ask your LLM to classify new queries. It's not fancy, but it works surprisingly well.

    One trick: always include an "other" category. New query types will emerge, and you need to catch them. When "other" grows above 10-15%, it's time to re-segment.

    Case Study: Construction Project Management

    Let me share a story that still makes me cringe. We were working with a construction project management company - big client, lots of pressure to deliver.

    Their RAG system searched across thousands of documents: contracts, schedules, budgets, permits, emails, everything. The metrics looked good:

    • 70% average satisfaction
    • 10,000 queries per week
    • Usage growing 15% month-over-month

    My client contact was happy. "See? We're doing great!"

    Then we segmented the data. What we found made my stomach drop.

    The Hidden Pattern

    Here's what was really happening:

    • Scheduling queries: 15% of volume, 35% satisfaction
    • Document search: 50% of volume, 85% satisfaction
    • Budget queries: 20% of volume, 75% satisfaction
    • Compliance: 15% of volume, 70% satisfaction

    But here's the kicker - when we looked at new users:

    • 80% started with scheduling queries
    • They failed miserably (35% success rate)
    • Then they adapted by using document search instead

    We were celebrating our document search success while completely failing new users on their first interaction. It's like having a restaurant where the appetizers are terrible but the dessert is great - sure, some people stick around for dessert, but most leave after the appetizer.

    The Survival Bias Trap

    We thought we were successful because we only measured the users who stuck around. The ones who left after failing to find schedule information? They were invisible in our "70% satisfaction" metric. Classic survivorship bias.

    The Investigation

    I spent a day watching users (with their permission). Here's what I saw:

    New project manager joins. First question: "When is the foundation pour scheduled?"

    System returns some vaguely related documents about concrete specifications. Useless.

    User tries again: "Foundation schedule"

    More random documents.

    User gives up and tries: "Find Johnson project schedule document"

    Success! They get the schedule PDF. But now they have to manually search through a 47-page document to find the foundation pour date.

    No wonder satisfaction was terrible. We were making them work around our system's failures.

    The Fix

    Instead of tweaking embeddings (my first instinct), we attacked the root cause:

    1. Built a schedule parser: Extracted dates, tasks, and dependencies from Gantt charts and project timelines
    2. Created structured data: Every schedule item became searchable - "foundation pour" → "March 15, 2024"
    3. Added a calendar view: Users could see schedules visually, not just as document results

    The results shocked everyone:

    • Scheduling satisfaction: 35% → 82%
    • New user retention: +40% (they stopped bouncing after the first query)
    • Overall satisfaction: 70% → 84%
    • Document search volume actually dropped 20% (users found what they needed directly)

    The lesson hit me hard: your best-performing segments might be compensating for failures elsewhere. Users are clever - they'll find workarounds. But workarounds aren't success stories, they're failure modes in disguise.

    Advanced Segmentation Techniques

    Once you've got the basics down, there are some more sophisticated approaches that can reveal hidden patterns.

    Multi-Dimensional Segmentation

    Here's a mistake I see all the time: people segment by topic OR by user type OR by complexity. Why not all three?

    The magic happens when you combine dimensions. Let me show you what I mean.

    At a financial services company, we segmented queries three ways:

    • Topic: Trading, compliance, research, account management
    • User role: Trader, analyst, compliance officer, manager
    • Complexity: Simple lookup, multi-step analysis, comparison

    When we looked at just topic, trading queries seemed fine - 75% satisfaction. But when we added user role, we discovered:

    • Traders asking trading questions: 89% satisfaction
    • Analysts asking trading questions: 45% satisfaction

    Same topic, completely different experience. Turns out traders wanted quick price checks while analysts needed deep historical analysis. We'd optimized for traders and left analysts in the dust.

    The Power of Multi-Dimensional Analysis

    By segmenting across topic × role × complexity, we found that "complex trading analysis for analysts" was our worst-performing segment (32% satisfaction) but also our highest-value one (these analysts influenced million-dollar decisions). One focused improvement drove more value than six months of general "AI improvements."

    Temporal Analysis: Patterns Over Time

    Your query patterns aren't static. They change, and these changes tell you important things.

    I learned this lesson the hard way at an e-commerce company. We noticed a steady increase in "return policy" queries. My first thought? Our return policy must be confusing.

    Wrong. When we dug deeper, we found the spike started exactly when we launched a new product line. The products were defective, and customers were trying to return them. The query pattern was a symptom, not the disease.

    Now I always track:

    • Emerging patterns: New query types growing >20% month-over-month
    • Seasonal patterns: Tax queries in March, budget queries in December
    • Event-driven patterns: Product launches, policy changes, outages

    One client discovered that "pricing calculator" queries spiked every Monday morning. Why? Their sales team was preparing weekly client proposals. We built a dedicated tool for Monday morning pricing calculations and cut support tickets by 40%.

    Cohort Analysis: The User Journey

    Here's something that blew my mind: users don't just have different needs, they have different needs at different times.

    Track cohorts of users from when they start using your system. You'll see fascinating patterns:

    Month 1: Basic questions ("How do I log in?", "Where is X feature?") Month 2-3: Intermediate usage ("How do I compare Y across Z?") Month 4+: Advanced workflows ("Generate a report showing...")

    At one company, we found that users who didn't advance beyond basic questions by month 2 had an 80% chance of churning. We built an onboarding flow specifically for stuck users, and retention improved by 25%.

    The key insight: Don't just segment by what users ask. Segment by when they ask it in their journey.

    Building Your Classification Pipeline

    Once you know your segments, you need to classify new queries in real-time. Here's how I approach it.

    The Simple Approach That Actually Works

    Forget complex ML pipelines for now. Start with a few-shot classifier. It's embarrassingly simple and surprisingly effective.

    Here's what you do:

    1. Collect good examples: Take 5-10 representative queries from each segment
    2. Build a prompt: Show the LLM your examples and ask it to classify new queries
    3. Add confidence scores: Ask the model how confident it is (this helps catch edge cases)
    4. Always include "other": New patterns will emerge - you need to catch them

    I've seen teams spend months building complex classification systems when a simple prompt would have worked fine. Don't be those teams.

    Making It Production-Ready

    Once the basic classifier works, you need to make it reliable:

    Caching is crucial: Same queries get asked repeatedly. Cache classifications for common queries. I've seen 10x performance improvements just from smart caching.

    Monitor for drift: Track your segment distributions over time. If "scheduling" queries suddenly jump from 15% to 40%, something changed. Maybe you launched a new feature, maybe something broke.

    The Automation Paradox: Here's something I read in an MBA book somewhere that really stuck with me - automation saves you time, but the issues will multiply if left unchecked. If you have a machine punch holes and it's off by an inch, if you don't check this over time, it's going to ruin a bunch of your production. The solution? High quality sampling at a regular cadence. As long as we're checking regularly, we're going to be okay.

    Set up alerts: When I was at that construction company, we set alerts for:

    • Any segment growing >50% week-over-week
    • "Other" category exceeding 15% (I generally want "other" to be around 10-20%, it will represent the long tail of queries that are not well covered by your system or are card to capture)
    • Confidence scores dropping below 70%

    These alerts saved us multiple times. Once, "budget" queries spiked 200% overnight. Turns out our finance team had sent an all-hands email about new budget procedures, driving everyone to the RAG system. We quickly added FAQs about the new process.

    The Feedback Loop

    Here's the beautiful part: classification creates a feedback loop.

    Every query gets classified → You see patterns → You improve the weak segments → User behavior changes → New patterns emerge → Repeat.

    I watched this happen at a legal tech company:

    1. We fixed contract search (their biggest pain point)
    2. Contract query volume dropped 30% (users found answers faster)
    3. Litigation queries increased 50% (users trusted the system more)
    4. We discovered litigation had terrible performance
    5. Fixed litigation, and the cycle continued

    Without classification, we'd never have seen these patterns. We'd still be randomly tweaking embeddings and hoping for the best.

    Common Patterns and Anti-Patterns

    After doing this dozens of times, I've seen the same patterns emerge. Learn from my mistakes.

    Pattern: The Learning Curve

    Users are smart. They adapt. Track how individual users' queries evolve and you'll see fascinating patterns.

    Here's a real example from a financial analytics platform:

    Week 1: "Show me Apple stock price" Week 2: "AAPL price last 30 days" Week 3: "AAPL vs MSFT performance" Week 4: "Technology sector momentum indicators"

    See what happened? The user learned what the system could do and asked progressively more sophisticated questions. This is great! It means they trust your system.

    But here's the flip side. When queries get simpler over time, you have a problem:

    Week 1: "Compare Q3 revenue across all product lines" Week 2: "Show Q3 revenue for Product A" Week 3: "Revenue Product A" Week 4: "financial data spreadsheet"

    This user gave up on complex queries and retreated to basic document search. They're working around your limitations.

    Track these patterns. They tell you more than any satisfaction score.

    Anti-Pattern: Over-Segmentation

    I consulted for a financial services company that went segment-crazy. They created 150+ query categories.

    The result? Complete paralysis:

    • Engineers didn't know where to start
    • Most segments had <10 queries per week
    • Nobody could remember what all the segments meant
    • Maintenance became a nightmare

    The Goldilocks Zone

    Too few segments (3-4) and you miss important patterns. Too many (50+) and you can't see the forest for the trees. The sweet spot is usually 10-20 segments that cover 80%+ of your queries. Everything else goes in "other."

    My rule: If you can't explain all your segments in a 5-minute meeting, you have too many.

    Pattern: Segment Migration

    Here's something wild - when you fix one segment, users' behavior changes across ALL segments.

    I saw this at an e-commerce company:

    1. Fixed product search (went from 40% to 85% satisfaction)
    2. Product search volume dropped 20% (users found things faster)
    3. Comparison queries jumped 60% (users trusted the system to do more)
    4. Customer service queries dropped 30% (users found answers themselves)

    One improvement cascaded through the entire system. This is why you need to monitor continuously - success in one area changes behavior everywhere.

    From Analysis to Action

    Alright, you've segmented your queries and found the problems. Now what? Here's my battle-tested approach to actually fixing things.

    1. Prioritize by Impact

    I use a simple formula that's saved me from countless bad decisions:

    Priority = Volume × Problem Size × Business Value

    Let me show you how this played out at a healthcare company:

    • Medication side effects: 5% volume, 40% satisfaction, critical for patient safety
    • Appointment scheduling: 30% volume, 60% satisfaction, nice-to-have feature
    • Insurance eligibility: 15% volume, 45% satisfaction, directly impacts revenue

    By the numbers, appointment scheduling looked like the biggest opportunity. But when we factored in business value (patient safety + revenue impact), the priorities completely flipped.

    We fixed insurance eligibility first. Why? Each failed insurance query meant a delayed procedure and lost revenue. The ROI was 10x higher than improving appointment scheduling.

    2. Set Measurable Goals

    This is where most teams screw up. They set vague goals like "improve search" or "make AI better."

    Goals That Actually Work

    ❌ Bad: "Improve scheduling queries"

    ✅ **Good:** "Increase scheduling query satisfaction from 35% to 70% within 6 weeks, measured by thumbs up/down feedback"

✅ **Even Better:** "Reduce 'schedule not found' errors from 45% to under 10% by parsing Gantt charts, measured by presence of date/time in response"

    The difference? Good goals tell you exactly what success looks like and how to measure it.

    3. Design Targeted Solutions

    Different problems need different medicine. Here's my mental model:

    If satisfaction is low because retrieval returns nothing:

    • Missing data → Add more documents
    • Wrong embeddings → Fine-tune for your domain
    • No metadata → Extract and index metadata

    If satisfaction is low despite good retrieval:

    • Wrong format → Build custom parsers
    • Missing capabilities → Add new tools/functions
    • Complex reasoning → Multi-step pipelines

    At that construction company, scheduling queries failed because we were doing embedding search on PDFs of Gantt charts. Embeddings can't read Gantt charts! We needed structured extraction, not better embeddings.

    4. Measure and Iterate

    Here's the part everyone forgets: checking if your fix actually worked.

    I was working with a legal tech company. We spent two weeks improving contract search, launched it, and celebrated. A week later, I checked the metrics. Satisfaction had barely moved.

    Why? We'd improved search for standard contracts, but users were mostly searching for amendments and addenda - totally different document types we hadn't considered.

    Always measure impact. And not just satisfaction - look at:

    • Did query volume change? (Maybe users trust the system more)
    • Did downstream metrics improve? (Support tickets, user retention)
    • Did behavior patterns shift? (Simple → complex queries)

    One client saw satisfaction improve only 10% but support tickets dropped 40%. The fix worked - users just weren't rating it higher.

    Conclusion: Data-Driven Improvement

    Here's what I want you to remember: aggregate metrics lie.

    Your 70% satisfaction score is meaningless if new users are failing 80% of the time on their first query. Your "good" performance might be hiding critical failures that are killing your business.

    Topic modeling and segmentation aren't fancy data science techniques - they're survival tools. They transform vague complaints ("search sucks") into specific, fixable problems ("date comparisons for financial data have 23% satisfaction").

    The four-quadrant framework I showed you? It ends arguments. No more debating what to build next. Just plot your segments and the priorities become obvious. High volume + low satisfaction = fix it now.

    And here's the thing I really want to stick in your mind: the next time someone says "make the AI better," I want you to think about what specific kinds of query types we might need to identify to double down on. The goal is that next time you stand up, instead of just coming back and saying "we need to make the AI better," you can identify which segment we need to improve by how much and what kind of experiments we need to get that done.

    But here's the real magic: this creates a flywheel. You fix one segment → users trust the system more → they ask harder questions → you see new patterns → you fix those → repeat.

    I've watched this transform companies. One client went from 50% satisfaction and declining usage to 85% satisfaction and 3x usage growth. Not through AI breakthroughs - through systematic improvement of specific segments.

    Reflection Questions

    1. If you looked at your last 1,000 queries right now, what patterns would jump out?

    2. Which user segments are probably working around your system's limitations?

    3. What would happen if your highest-volume query type suddenly had 90% satisfaction?

    4. How would your roadmap change if you could see exactly which improvements would impact which users?

    5. What behavioral patterns in your system might reveal problems your metrics are hiding?

    Summary

    Aggregate metrics hide the real problems. Your 70% satisfaction might conceal that new users fail 80% of the time, or that your biggest customers hate your system. Topic modeling reveals these hidden patterns by grouping similar queries and showing you exactly where you're failing. The four-quadrant framework (volume vs satisfaction) makes prioritization obvious - fix high-volume, low-satisfaction segments first.

    Real examples prove this works: a construction company discovered users were adapting to scheduling failures by using document search. Fixing scheduling improved retention 40%. Users are clever - they work around your failures. These workarounds aren't success stories, they're hidden failure modes.

    Start simple: read queries manually, use basic clustering, build a few-shot classifier. Monitor how segments change over time. User behavior evolves - what works today might fail tomorrow. Most importantly: one improvement cascades through your entire system. Fix scheduling, and suddenly document search, comparisons, and support queries all change. That's the power of systematic improvement.

    Key Takeaways

    1. Your Average User Doesn't Exist: You have segments with completely different needs and success rates. Find them.

    2. The Four-Quadrant Magic: High volume + low satisfaction = your hair-on-fire emergency. Fix it first.

    3. Users Adapt to Failure: When complex queries fail, users retreat to simple ones. Track this behavioral pattern.

    4. Start Manual, Then Automate: Read 200 queries before you touch any ML. Patterns will jump out.

    5. 10-20 Segments is the Sweet Spot: Too few and you miss nuances. Too many and you're paralyzed.

    6. One Fix Changes Everything: Improve one segment and watch behavior shift across all segments.

    7. Business Value Matters: 5% of queries from regulators might matter more than 50% from regular users.
