    Prioritization and Roadmapping: From Insights to Action

    The Expected Value Framework

    Alright, so you've segmented your queries and found a dozen problems. Your engineering team is asking "What do we build first?" Here's the framework I use with every client:

    Expected Value = Impact Volume (1 - Current Success Rate)

    This isn't some theoretical BS. This is how you stop wasting months on the wrong things.

    Let me tell you about a painful lesson I learned. We spent 3 months building this beautiful multi-document comparison feature for a legal tech company. The engineering was impressive - it could compare dozens of contracts simultaneously, highlight differences, track changes over time.

    Usage after launch? 2% of queries. Impact on satisfaction? Basically zero.

    Meanwhile, 40% of users were failing to find basic clause definitions because our date filter was broken. We could have fixed that in a week.

    The Shiny Object Syndrome

    I see this constantly: teams get excited about cool AI features while ignoring boring problems that actually matter. That fancy chat interface you're building? Users would trade it in a heartbeat for working date filters.

    Inventory vs Capabilities: The Critical Distinction

    Here's the thing that took me way too long to understand: there are only two reasons your RAG system fails. Just two. Once you know which one you're dealing with, the fix becomes obvious.

    Lack of Inventory: You Don't Have What They Want

    This is like running a bookstore and someone asks for cookbooks, but you only stock mysteries. Doesn't matter how good your search is - you can't find what doesn't exist.

    I learned this lesson at a healthcare startup. Doctors kept searching for "2024 hypertension guidelines" and getting nothing. We spent weeks tweaking embeddings, adjusting chunk sizes, trying different models.

    You know what the problem was? We hadn't ingested the 2024 guidelines. They'd been published three months earlier and nobody had added them to our corpus.

    How to spot inventory problems: - Your retrieval returns nothing or garbage - Cosine similarity scores are consistently low - The LLM literally says "I cannot find information about..." - Users get desperate and try increasingly generic searches

    Real examples I've seen: - Legal firms missing recent case law (published last month) - HR systems without the latest policy updates (CEO announced them, but they're not in the system) - Customer support missing docs for features that shipped yesterday

    The fix is usually embarrassingly simple: add the missing data. But you'd be amazed how often teams try everything else first.

    Lack of Capabilities: You Have It But Can't Use It

    This one's trickier. The information exists in your system, but you can't surface it the way users need.

    My favorite example: a financial services company where users kept asking "What's the revenue difference between Q3 and Q4?" The system had both quarters' data, beautifully embedded and indexed. But it couldn't do math across documents.

    Users would get Q3 revenue in one response, Q4 revenue in another, then break out a calculator. We had the ingredients but couldn't make the meal.

    How to spot capability problems: - High retrieval scores but low satisfaction - Users rephrase the same question multiple ways - The right documents are found but the answer is wrong - Common patterns like "compare X and Y" or "show changes since Z"

    The pattern I see constantly: - No date filtering "What changed last week?" fails - No comparison logic "How does A compare to B?" fails
    - No aggregation "Total across all departments?" fails - No structured extraction Tables and charts might as well not exist

    The DoorDash Revelation

    My favorite inventory story: DoorDash analyzed failing searches and found "Greek food near me" had 8% success rate. The problem wasn't AI - they literally had three Greek restaurants in the entire city.

    What did they do? Used the search data to convince Greek restaurant owners to join the platform. They even bought iPads for restaurants that couldn't afford the tech. Search failure data literally drove business development.

    Six months later? "Greek food near me" had 75% satisfaction. Not through AI improvements - through fixing the inventory problem.

    The Expected Value Framework in Practice

    Let me show you exactly how this played out at that construction company I mentioned. Here's what our analysis revealed:

    Schedule queries: 15% of volume, 35% success rate, critical for project managers Document search: 50% of volume, 85% success rate, general purpose tool Budget queries: 20% of volume, 75% success rate, important for executives Compliance: 15% of volume, 70% success rate, needed for audits

    By the numbers, document search looked like our winner - highest volume, great success rate. Case closed, right?

    Wrong. Dead wrong.

    The Hidden Pattern That Changes Everything

    Here's what the aggregate numbers didn't show:

    When we tracked new users specifically, 80% started with scheduling queries. "When is the foundation pour?" "What's on the critical path?" "Show me next week's deliveries."

    They failed miserably. 35% success rate.

    Then we noticed something fascinating. These same users would come back and search for "Johnson project schedule PDF" or "March timeline document." Document search had 85% success because users learned to work around our failure.

    The Workaround Trap

    We were celebrating 85% success on document search while new users were learning our system was useless for its primary purpose. It's like a restaurant celebrating that customers love the free bread while the main courses are inedible.

    The real priority became crystal clear: fix scheduling first. Not because it had the highest expected value on paper, but because it was killing our new user experience. Every new project manager's first experience with our system was failure.

    Building Your Roadmap: A Systematic Approach

    After years of doing this wrong, here's the approach that actually works.

    Step 1: The Diagnosis Game

    For each broken segment, ask yourself: - Is it inventory or capability? (Usually takes 5 minutes to figure out) - How hard is it to fix? (Hours? Days? Weeks?) - Who's screaming loudest? (Your biggest customer? New users? The CEO?) - What else breaks if we fix this? (Dependencies are roadmap killers)

    I use a simple spreadsheet. Nothing fancy. Segment, problem type, effort, impact, dependencies. That's it.

    Step 2: Find Your Power Law

    Here's what I've learned from dozens of implementations: - 20% of your segments drive 80% of value - 20% of fixes will solve 80% of complaints - 20% of users create 80% of support tickets

    Your job? Find that 20%.

    At an e-commerce company, we discovered that "compare prices across sellers" was 8% of queries but generated 60% of revenue. Why? These were high-intent buyers making purchase decisions. One segment, massive impact.

    Step 3: Sequence Like a Chess Player

    Order matters more than you think. Here's my playbook:

    Start with Quick Wins Why? Because nothing builds trust like fast results.

    I always find 2-3 things we can fix in the first week: - Missing documents (just add them) - Broken date parsing (usually a regex fix) - Better error messages (users love knowing why something failed)

    These seem trivial, but they prove you're listening. I've seen satisfaction jump 10% from error messages alone.

    Build Platform Capabilities Next The temptation is to jump straight to segment-specific fixes. Resist it.

    Platform capabilities compound. Build date filtering once, every segment benefits. Add comparison logic once, unlock dozens of use cases.

    At that construction company: - Week 1: Fixed "last week" date parsing (helped 4 segments) - Week 2: Added document metadata extraction (helped 6 segments) - Week 3: Built comparison framework (helped 3 segments)

    By week 4, half our problems were already solved by platform improvements.

    Follow the User Journey Fix problems in the order users encounter them. New user onboarding failures before power user features. Common workflows before edge cases.

    A Roadmap That Actually Shipped

    Here's a real 6-week roadmap we executed:

    Week 1: Trust Building - Added 2023 tax documents (inventory) - 2 hours - Fixed "this month" date parsing - 4 hours
    - Better "no results" messages - 1 day Result: 8% satisfaction bump, team morale through the roof

    Week 2-3: Platform Power - Metadata extraction for all PDFs - 1 week - Universal date/time filtering - 3 days - Document comparison framework - 3 days Result: Enabled fixes for 5 different segments

    Week 4-6: Segment Specific - Schedule parsing from Gantt charts - 1 week - Budget rollup across projects - 4 days - Compliance checklist generator - 3 days Result: 35% 78% satisfaction on critical segments

    Total impact: 70% 84% overall satisfaction, 40% reduction in support tickets

    Common Capability Patterns

    After seeing the same problems at company after company, I started keeping a list. Here are the capabilities that come up everywhere:

    Temporal Capabilities (The Time Problem)

    This is the big one. I'd estimate 40% of capability failures involve time somehow.

    Everyone wants: - "Show me last week's changes" - "Compare Q3 to Q4"
    - "What's due by Friday?" - "What's new since my last login?"

    Sounds simple, right? But "last week" might mean: - Previous calendar week (Sunday-Saturday) - Previous 7 days - Previous business week (Monday-Friday) - Previous fiscal week (companies are weird)

    I once spent two days debugging why "last month" queries were failing in January. Turns out, users saying "last month" in January meant December of the previous year. Our system was looking for month 0.

    Comparison Capabilities (The "Versus" Problem)

    Second most common. Users love comparing things: - "Budget vs actual" - "This year vs last year" - "Our performance vs competitors" - "Version 2 vs version 3"

    The trap: you need the same extraction logic applied to both things being compared. If your budget extraction pulls different fields than your actuals extraction, the comparison is meaningless.

    Aggregation Capabilities (The "Total" Problem)

    CFOs love totals. "What's our total exposure across all contracts?" "Sum of all budget overruns?" "Average processing time by department?"

    Here's what trips people up: aggregation across documents is fundamentally different from search. Search finds documents. Aggregation does math on what's inside them.

    I watched a team spend months trying to make embeddings understand SUM(). Embeddings don't do math. Build a separate aggregation system.

    Extraction Capabilities (The Structure Problem)

    PDFs are where data goes to die. Tables, forms, charts - all locked in visual formats that embeddings can't parse.

    My running list of extraction nightmares: - Tables split across pages - Footnotes that contain critical data - Charts where the legend is more important than the chart - Forms where the field names are images

    One client had financial data in PDFs where negative numbers were shown in red. The text extraction saw "1000" not "-1000". Their totals were off by millions until we added color detection.

    Monitoring Your Roadmap Impact

    Here's another painful lesson: most teams launch improvements and never check if they worked. Don't be those teams.

    Leading Indicators (The Early Warning System)

    These tell you immediately if you're on the right track:

    Query volume changes: When you fix something, usage should go up. At that construction company, scheduling queries jumped 300% after we fixed them. Why? Users who'd given up came back.

    Retry patterns: Track how often users rephrase queries. Good fixes see retry rates plummet. I've seen retry rates drop from 60% to 15% just by adding date filtering.

    Time to success: How long until users get what they need? We measure this religiously. One client saw average session time drop from 12 minutes to 3 minutes after fixing comparison queries.

    The query death spiral: Bad segment User tries Fails Tries simpler query Fails Gives up. Track this pattern. When it stops happening, you've truly fixed something.

    Lagging Indicators (The Report Card)

    These take longer to show up but matter more:

    Satisfaction by segment: Not overall satisfaction - that lies. Track each segment separately. You want to see steady improvement in the segments you fixed.

    Support ticket themes: The ultimate truth teller. When tickets about "can't find schedules" drop to zero, you've won.

    User retention: This is the big one. At a legal tech company, fixing contract search improved 30-day retention by 25%. One fix, massive business impact.

    The Beautiful Chaos of Success

    Here's something nobody tells you: successful improvements create chaos. But it's good chaos.

    When we fixed scheduling at that construction company: 1. Scheduling queries increased 300% (users trusted it again) 2. Comparison queries emerged ("compare planned vs actual schedule") 3. New feature requests appeared ("can you send alerts for delays?") 4. Document search dropped 20% (users found what they needed directly)

    Every fix changes the entire system. Your roadmap isn't a straight line - it's a living document that evolves with user behavior.

    The Cascade Effect

    A B2B SaaS company fixed date filtering. Within two weeks: - Date-based queries increased 400% - Comparison queries doubled ("this month vs last month") - Export requests tripled (users trusted the data) - Their largest customer expanded their contract

    One platform capability unlocked an entire new usage pattern. That's the power of fixing the right thing.

    The Portfolio Approach

    I learned this from a VP of Product who used to work at a VC firm. Treat your roadmap like an investment portfolio:

    10% Moonshots The crazy ideas that might change everything. We once built a system that automatically generated FAQ documents from failed queries. Weird idea. Took one engineer two weeks. Reduced support tickets by 30%.

    Most moonshots fail. That's fine. The ones that work pay for all the failures.

    70% Core Improvements The bread and butter. Fixing known problems, adding requested features, improving what exists. Not sexy, but this is what actually moves the needle day-to-day.

    These should be predictable. "Add date filtering" isn't going to surprise you. It'll work about as well as you expect.

    20% Infrastructure The stuff nobody asks for but everyone needs. Better monitoring, faster deployments, cleaner code. This is what prevents your system from collapsing under its own weight.

    I've seen teams go 100% features and then spend months firefighting because they can't even tell what's broken.

    Portfolio in Practice

    Real roadmap from a healthcare startup:

    Moonshot: Auto-generate patient summaries from conversation history Core: Fix medication search (40% of queries), add dosage calculations, improve date filtering Infrastructure: Query classification system, performance monitoring dashboard

    The moonshot failed spectacularly. But the infrastructure we built for it powered three other features later.

    Communicating Your Roadmap

    Here's my secret weapon: data kills arguments.

    For Executives (They Want Business Impact)

    "We're fixing scheduling because: - 1,500 queries per week are failing - 80% of new users encounter this first - Fixing it increases retention by 40% - ROI: 3 months of work = $2M annual revenue impact"

    Notice what I didn't say? Nothing about embeddings, models, or technical details. They don't care.

    For Engineers (They Want Technical Clarity)

    "Temporal filtering is our next platform capability: - Solves problems in 5 different segments - One implementation, multiple uses - Clear interfaces and test cases - Enables future comparison features"

    Engineers want to know it's well-thought-out and won't be throwaway work.

    For Users (They Want Hope)

    "Based on your feedback, coming next month: Working date filters ('last week' will actually work!) Schedule search that finds actual dates Budget comparisons across projects

    Thanks for your patience while we fix these issues."

    Users want to know you heard them and you're fixing their specific problems.

    Advanced Prioritization Techniques

    After years of painful lessons, here are the traps to avoid:

    The Complexity Cliff

    Some "simple" fixes are icebergs. We once estimated "add date filtering" at one week. Three months later we were still handling edge cases.

    Hidden complexity I've hit: - "Last quarter" in January (which year?) - Time zones (whose midnight?) - Fiscal years (why does your year start in July?) - Historical changes (when did the fiscal year change?)

    Now I always ask: "What's the weirdest edge case?" If the answer is longer than a paragraph, triple your estimate.

    The Network Effect

    Some improvements are force multipliers. They make everything else better.

    Best example: metadata extraction at a legal firm. We thought it would help search. But once we had extracted metadata: - Filtering became trivial - Comparisons suddenly worked - Aggregations were possible - Compliance reports automated themselves

    One platform investment unlocked dozens of features. Always look for these multiplier effects.

    The Technical Debt Trap

    I consulted for a company with 47 different query handlers. Forty. Seven. Each one solved a specific problem. Each one was maintained separately. Each one broke differently.

    Their deploys took 3 hours. Bug fixes took weeks. New engineers took months to onboard.

    The Point of No Return

    Once you have more than 10-15 segment-specific solutions, maintenance becomes your full-time job. You stop building features and start playing whack-a-mole with bugs. I've seen teams completely paralyzed by their own "success."

    Case Studies in Prioritization

    Let me share three stories where the obvious priority was completely wrong:

    Financial Services: The $10M Edge Case

    The data said focus on trading queries - 40% of volume, decent satisfaction, used by everyone.

    Then we dug deeper. Compliance queries were only 5% of volume. But they came from regulators. One failed query during an audit = potential $10M fine.

    We dropped everything and fixed compliance. The head of compliance literally hugged me when it shipped. Sometimes impact isn't measured in volume.

    Healthcare: The Beautiful Collapse

    Hospital system came to us with three "separate" problems: - Medication dosing lookups failing - Drug interaction checks broken - Insurance formulary searches useless

    Three teams were planning three solutions. Then someone noticed: all three were querying the same medication database, just asking different questions.

    We built one medication knowledge graph with three query interfaces. Triple win with one-third the effort. Always look for the underlying pattern.

    E-commerce: The Market Research Goldmine

    Fashion retailer had terrible search satisfaction for: - "Sustainable materials" - "Size inclusive options" - "Local designers"

    They wanted better search algorithms. But when we looked at the data... they had exactly 3 sustainable items, no plus sizes, and zero local designers.

    The search queries became their product roadmap. Within 6 months, those were their fastest-growing categories. Sometimes your RAG system is trying to tell you about your business.

    From Roadmap to Reality

    A roadmap without execution is just wishful thinking. Here's how to actually ship:

    Start Small, Show Value

    Pick one thing. Fix it completely. Measure the impact. Tell everyone.

    I always start with something I can fix in a week. Why? Because nothing kills momentum like a 3-month project that might work. Ship something in week one, and suddenly everyone believes.

    Real example: Added "yesterday" date parsing. Took 4 hours. Satisfaction for date queries jumped 15%. CEO mentioned it in the all-hands. Team was pumped.

    Build Your Feedback Loops

    You need to know immediately if something's working: - Daily metrics dashboard (I check mine with coffee) - Weekly segment reviews (15 minutes, just the highlights) - Monthly deep dives (what really changed?) - Quarterly strategy adjustments (throw out what's not working)

    The mistake I see: teams ship features and check metrics monthly. By then, you've wasted weeks heading the wrong direction.

    Maintain Momentum

    Momentum is everything. Lose it, and your roadmap becomes shelfware.

    My tricks: - Ship something visible every 2 weeks - Celebrate wins publicly (Slack, email, wherever) - Show before/after metrics constantly - Let engineers present their fixes

    One client created a "Fixed That!" Slack channel. Every improvement got posted with metrics. Best team morale booster I've ever seen.

    Stay Flexible (But Not Too Flexible)

    Your roadmap will change. Users will surprise you. New problems will emerge. That's fine.

    But don't chase every shiny object. I use a simple rule: we can change direction monthly, not daily. Gives enough flexibility without whiplash.

    When Flexibility Pays Off

    Healthcare startup planned to fix medication search. Week 2, COVID hit. Suddenly everyone needed vaccine information. We pivoted, built vaccine search in 10 days, became the go-to tool for clinicians. Sometimes the world changes your roadmap for you.

    Conclusion: Strategy Beats Tactics

    Here's what 10 years of RAG consulting taught me:

    The teams that win don't have better AI. They have better strategies. They know exactly which problems to solve, in what order, and why.

    The expected value framework turns arguments into math. Inventory vs capability turns confusion into clarity. Platform improvements turn point solutions into scalable systems.

    But most importantly: your users are telling you what to build. Every failed query, every workaround, every support ticket - it's all product direction. You just have to organize it and listen.

    I've watched teams go from "our search sucks" to "our users love us" in 3 months. Not through AI breakthroughs. Through systematic, data-driven improvement.

    Your roadmap isn't just a plan - it's your path from random feature building to strategic product development.

    Remember: Perfect solutions for edge cases < Good solutions for common problems. Every time.

    Reflection Questions

    1. Look at your last quarter's work. What percentage went to your highest-impact segments?

    2. How many of your current problems are actually inventory issues in disguise?

    3. What platform capability would eliminate half your segment-specific work?

    4. If you could only fix three things this quarter, what would move the business metrics?

    5. What feedback signals are you ignoring because they're inconvenient?

    Summary

    Stop building random features. Start solving specific problems for specific segments.

    The expected value framework (Impact Volume Success) turns subjective debates into objective priorities. Just do the math. But watch for hidden patterns - like new users failing on common tasks while your averages look fine.

    Diagnose before you build. Inventory problems need data. Capability problems need features. Building features for inventory problems is like adding better search to an empty library.

    Platform improvements compound. Date filtering helps five segments. Better parsing helps ten. Segment-specific fixes help one. Build platforms first, features second.

    Your roadmap is a living document. Every improvement changes user behavior. Success creates new demands. Monitor, adapt, but don't thrash. Monthly pivots, not daily.

    Most importantly: communicate with data. "Fix scheduling queries failing for 80% of new users" beats "improve AI" every time. Numbers end arguments and align teams.

    Key Takeaways

    1. Do the Math: Expected Value = Impact Volume (1 - Success Rate). No opinions, just numbers.

    2. Diagnose First: Inventory problems Capability problems. Wrong diagnosis = wasted effort.

    3. New Users Matter Most: They haven't learned your workarounds yet. Fix their experience first.

    4. Platform Beats Features: One date parser helps everyone. One segment fix helps... one segment.

    5. Quick Wins Build Trust: Ship something meaningful in week one. Momentum matters.

    6. Success Changes Everything: Fixed segments see 3-5x usage increase. Plan for the chaos.

    7. Talk Their Language: Executives want impact. Engineers want clarity. Users want hope. Give them what they need.
